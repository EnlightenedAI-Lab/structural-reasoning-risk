What the Instrument Measures

The instrument converts an AI-generated analysis into an inspectable structure: claims, premises, and conclusions, plus the support links between them. It then quantifies whether the output is structurally justified. These signals are designed for governance workflows where auditability matters.

Unit of Analysis

  • Claim: a distinct assertion that must be supported.
  • Premise: an asserted reason or evidence offered in support.
  • Conclusion: a commitment that depends on premises (often implicit).
  • Support link: a traceable dependency from premise → claim/conclusion.
The goal is not to “grade writing.” The goal is to make support structure visible and measurable.

Explicit Non-Goals

  • No factual truth verification.
  • No sentiment, bias, or ideology scoring.
  • No policy merit judgment (“good/bad idea”).
  • No prediction of outcomes.
This instrument measures derivability from stated premises, not whether the premises are true.

Core Structural Signals

Below are the primary signals used to detect unsupported commitment and structural vulnerability. Each signal is defined in plain language and mapped to the failure mode it detects.

Integrity Score Structural justification

What it is: a composite measure of how consistently conclusions follow from stated premises.

Detects: persuasive conclusions with insufficient support; “looks right” outputs that do not logically derive.

Decision implication: low integrity → require manual review, deny escalation, or request missing evidence.

Premise Coverage Support completeness

What it is: how fully claims are supported by explicit premises in the text.

Detects: missing links; implied evidence; “hand-wavy” support.

Decision implication: low coverage → the document is structurally incomplete for audit-grade decisions.

Orphan Claim Rate Unsupported assertions

What it is: proportion of claims/conclusions with no traceable supporting premises.

Detects: unsupported commitments; hidden leaps; “because I say so” reasoning.

Decision implication: high orphan rate → high risk of committing to conclusions that cannot be defended later.

Reasoning Density Support per token

What it is: how much structurally relevant support exists relative to the length of the output.

Detects: verbose outputs with little actual justification; “style masking.”

Decision implication: low density → the output may sound thorough but contains little defensible structure.

Premise Depth Support chain depth

What it is: how many levels of support exist (premise → sub-premise → conclusion).

Detects: shallow justification; conclusions supported by generic statements rather than evidence chains.

Decision implication: shallow depth → request substantiation, sources, or external verification.

Support Concentration Single-point failure

What it is: whether many conclusions depend on a small set of premises.

Detects: fragile reasoning; one weak premise collapses the entire argument.

Decision implication: high concentration → prioritize verifying the few load-bearing premises.

Stability Under Variation Robustness

What it is: whether structure and conclusions remain consistent under controlled perturbations (e.g., mild prompt changes).

Detects: drift; reasoning that is not reproducible.

Decision implication: low stability → do not rely on the output for commitments; require additional runs or independent review.

Cross-Model Consensus Spread Comparability

What it is: how much models agree or diverge structurally under identical constraints.

Detects: false confidence; model-specific vulnerabilities; “agreement” that is only stylistic.

Decision implication: large spread → treat as high uncertainty; investigate premises and failure modes.

Hallucination Risk Unsupported fabrication

What it is: a structural estimate of the likelihood that content was introduced without evidentiary grounding.

Detects: invented support; fabricated details; hidden premise insertion.

Decision implication: elevated risk → require citations, evidence packet, or exclude unsupported portions.

Interpreting these signals is straightforward: the instrument tells you where support is missing and which premises are load-bearing. This is the difference between “it sounds right” and “it is defensible.”
